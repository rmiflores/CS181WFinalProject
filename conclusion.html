CS 181W Final Project: The Value of Privacy

By Ramon Flores, Daniel Liem, Ryan Perez, and John Wu


Privacy from a Utilitarian Perspective

Do each of these countries' policies provide the greatest good for the greatest amount of people?

United States

  • The United States is divided on the direction it wishes to advance towards in terms of cyber privacy

  • If implemented as planned, these attempted bills should increase utility as the threat of cybercrime would decrease, and no innocent’s privacy would be violated

  • However, it is impossible to say now if the laws would be exploitative or not, and if they were, the privacy loss of plentiful citizens decreases the overall utility exponentially.


India

  • The Indian government and people both realize the important of personal security, but the government does a poor job in satisfying the needs to the people

  • The government draws an ambiguous line between what should be accessed or not

  • The overall utility is decreased because the Indian people have become unnecessarily cautious about what they post due to its ability to be accessed


South Korea

  • The government has full access and is constantly regulating the internet, and the majority of the public feels safe; they accept having the government control privacy

  • In terms of a Utilitarian’s perspective: less privacy is better for Korea, keeping the country very regulated. The more government involvement, the better


China

  • China places limits on the privacy of netizens to retain a closer grip on maintaining political stability

  • Ideally, should lead to increased utility due to less conflict as a whole

  • In reality, lowers individual utility by reducing tendencies to speak due to fear of prosecution from the government, lowering the likelihood of whistleblowing too
